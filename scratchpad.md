4. Lots of content on what is the future going to be.
Superforecasters - you cannot predict the future for more than 5 years in advance. And 5 years is really a stretch.
If anyone tells you otherwise, and they're sure, they are either misleading, have a vested interest in the outcome, or do not understand their domain.
It's like in software engineering: you always have options, pros and cons. You never tell: go with Azure or AWS or GCP. Or JavaScript or Rust. It all depends.
The only exception: there will be AN EVENT, previously unpredictable:
- ChatGPT release
- COVID-19
- Putin's invasion of Ukraine

5. We are always told that new technology will:
- Either kill us.
- Or at the very least take our jobs.
And so far, this has never been true. We always had more jobs thanks to new technologies. We don't always see that at first:
- The first computer, developed in the 1940s by Alan Turing, faced a lot of opposition.
- Space flights caused massive protests.
- GPS for civil use was a gimmick.
And they all turned out to be great.
Sometimes the job we did were different, but the end result was the same: more jobs, better paid in better conditions.
The network admins -> SRE / DevOps / Cloud Engineers pivot.
And there's vastly more jobs for them today than there were back then.

6. Why is that?
- The evolutionary lens.
- The first ever recorded fearmongering about new technology was in ancient Greece - Plato in Phaedrus warned us that the prolifiration of writing would kill our memory.
  - As we know, exactly the opposite happened.
  - And this is also why I'm not really afraid of "some of the brightest minds in the world" being afraid that AI will be the end of us. Andrzej Dragan is not smarter than Plato. Even geniuses get it wrong.
- The whole thing accelerated since the first industrial revolution.
- Sometimes fearmongering can be quite funny:
- The example with toilets in Warsaw agglomeration.
  - As we know, exactly the opposite happened.

7. If someone is pessimistic about the future, we naturally assume they're more intelligent.
If someone is optimistic about the future, we naturally assume they're more stupid.
We have an inherent negativity bias.
Factulness described that in detail - take the story of a late 1940s Japanese person's example.

Be ahead of the curve and thanks to that:

- You may become an AI leader in your organization (either an official one or a de-facto one)

2. Promote internal knowledge sharing.
- Open a slack channel.
- Do tech talks.
- Create a prompt library.
- Btw. Pro/anti-AI polarization and unclear norms inhibit teams from developing shared AI "workflows"
This is how you get ahead.

3. Do not jump to any new tool or model.
Especially model. (ref: https://grid-box.enterprise.slack.com/archives/C08LMLYJNPQ/p1764154217535169)
Focus on building and shippint features. Don't even bother with novelties if you don't have the time and don't have FOMO. Just wait until there's enough singnal that it's good.

4. If you can, go work at a company which embraces AI and there is top-down support for it.
Like Box :)
